Common Password attacks

-Brute Force
Brute force attacks are a tried-and-true method that involves an exhaustive, systematic effort to guess a password.


- Dictionary attacks
Dictionary attacks represent a more targeted approach compared to brute-force tactics. Instead of attempting every conceivable combination, attackers utilize a curated list of probable passwords, often compiled from dictionaries, known leaks, or common password conventions.

- Phishing
Typically posing as a trusted entity, attackers craft misleading emails or messages that prompt users to enter sensitive information into a fraudulent website that mimics a legitimate service.


- Keylogger attacks 
Keylogger attacks are particularly insidious as they operate quietly in the background, capturing every keystroke made on a compromised machine. This information often includes sensitive data like usernames and passwords, which the attacker can later use or sell.


- Credential stuffing
Credential stuffing exploits password reuse by testing stolen username/password combinations across multiple websites.


- Mitm
Man in the middle attacks are when a hacker or compromised system sits in between two uncompromised people or systems and deciphers the information they're passing to each other, including passwords.



==================



Password complexity can improve security by making passwords harder to guess or crack, especially against brute force and dictionary attacks. But beyond a point, overly strict complexity rules can actually reduce security because users choose predictable patterns.
Even though we've been encouraged to use complex passwords, a long passphrase that is unique to one account is usually stronger than a short, highly complex password. Since a lot of passwords are leaked through data breeches rather than attacks, the point to keeping a unique password for different accounts stands true.



Some practices to make strong passwords:

~ Make It Long
~ Use a Mix of Characters 
~ Use different passwords for different accounts
~ Avoid using common passwords like abc123 etc
~ Avoid using your personal information (name, birthday, phone no)
~ Enable Multi-Factor Authentication
